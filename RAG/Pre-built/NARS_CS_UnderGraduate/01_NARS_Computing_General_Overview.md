## 1. Introduction

Computing and Information is concerned with the understanding, design, programming, and exploitation of computation and computer technology. It blends theories (derived from mathematics, engineering, psychology, graphical design) with the solution of practical problems. It supports both small-scale and large-scale systems that support organizational goals.

**Why Computing and Information Matters:**
- Part of everything we do in the 21st century
- Enables graduates to solve complex, challenging problems
- Drives innovation in sciences, engineering, business, entertainment and education
- Offers highly paid careers with strong job satisfaction and global demand
- Provides a foundation of knowledge, problem solving and logical thinking
- Has space for both collaborative work and individual effort

## 4. Common Requirements of All Computing and Information Degrees

All reputable computing and information degree programs must include:

1. **Essentials of the discipline** — foundations that remain unaltered despite technological change (formal theory, professional values and principles)

2. **Foundation in computer programming** (5 layers):
   - Intellectual understanding of mathematics, algorithms and data structures
   - Understanding of computer hardware from a software perspective
   - Fundamental programming skills
   - Skills to design and implement larger structural units with interfaces
   - Software engineering principles to ensure robust, reliable implementations

3. **Understanding possibilities and limitations** of computer technology at three levels:
   - What current technologies can and cannot accomplish
   - Inherent limitations vs. future capabilities
   - Impact on individuals, organizations and society

4. **Understanding of the lifecycle** — planning, development, deployment, evolution — and the relationship between quality and lifecycle management

5. **Understanding of process** — both in computing (program execution, system operation) and in professional activity (product quality and human processes)

6. **Study of advanced topics** — learning experiences leading from elementary to cutting-edge topics

7. **Non-technical skill sets** — interpersonal communication, team skills, management skills

8. **Exposure to applications and case studies** — connecting theory to real-world occurrences

9. **Professional, legal, and ethical issues** — developing attitudes that honor the profession's ethical stature

10. **Capstone project** — demonstrating integration of the undergraduate experience
